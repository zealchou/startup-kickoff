# Diagnostic Overlays (D1–D3)

> **These overlays do not score.** They do not feed the Survival Index denominator and they never trigger a Kill. They run once, after the 12 scored gauntlet rings, and are reported as an independent layer — a body-composition scan next to the pass/fail exam. Their job is to surface risk that a scoring rubric would flatten: structural business health, human/equity exposure, and strategic posture. Present all three even when the venture scored well; a 🏆 verdict with an unaddressed red flag in D2 or D3 still ships with a warning label.

---

## D1 — Business Physique Radar

A 12-dimension scan of the operating muscles a venture will eventually need, whether or not it needs them on day one. The point is not to demand strength everywhere immediately — it is to make the current shape visible, and to name which weaknesses are survivable now versus which will become load-bearing failures later.

For each dimension: a definition, three self-check questions, and three anchor descriptions (Strong / Middling / Weak). Score each dimension informally on the same anchors if a visual radar is being produced — but do not fold this into the Survival Index.

### 1. Product & Differentiation
Core value delivered and how distinct it is from the next-best alternative.
- Self-check: If a user described what we do to a friend without our name attached, would it sound different from three competitors?
- Self-check: What would break for the user if this product vanished tomorrow — and how fast?
- Self-check: Is the differentiation defensible, or is it a feature a well-funded competitor could ship in a quarter?
- **Strong**: A specific user segment can articulate, unprompted, what makes this different and why that difference matters to them.
- **Middling**: The differentiation is real but requires explanation; users like it once told, but don't discover it themselves.
- **Weak**: The pitch relies on adjectives ("better," "easier," "smarter") with no falsifiable claim behind them.

### 2. Compounding Assets
Cases, testimonials, usage data, and other proof-of-work that accumulate in value the longer the venture operates.
- Self-check: What do we have today that a same-day competitor entering the market could not replicate in a week?
- Self-check: Is our data/evidence store actually being used to make decisions, or just stored?
- Self-check: If we stopped selling for six months, would this asset still be worth something?
- **Strong**: A documented, growing library of evidence (cases, data, testimonials) that measurably improves conversion or product decisions.
- **Middling**: Some proof exists but is scattered, undocumented, or not reused systematically.
- **Weak**: No structured record of past wins; every sales conversation starts from zero.

### 3. Partnership & Integration
Ability to combine resources with other organizations — cross-industry tie-ups, channel partnerships, strategic alliances.
- Self-check: Name one partner whose customer base overlaps ours and who has an incentive to introduce us to it.
- Self-check: What would we have to give up to make that partnership attractive to them?
- Self-check: Do we have any live integration or agreement today, or only conversations?
- **Strong**: At least one active partnership generating measurable referral or distribution volume.
- **Middling**: Partnership conversations are underway but nothing is signed or live.
- **Weak**: No partner outreach has happened; the venture assumes it will grow entirely through owned channels.

### 4. Referral & Revenue-share Mechanics
Structured mechanisms for existing users or partners to bring in new ones — referral programs, revenue-share, licensing, co-creation entry points.
- Self-check: If our best customer wanted to refer someone today, is there a designated path for them to do it?
- Self-check: Is there any incentive (financial or status) for referring, or does it rely purely on goodwill?
- Self-check: Can we attribute any current revenue to a referral mechanism, or is all growth paid or organic-direct?
- **Strong**: A working referral or revenue-share loop with measurable attribution.
- **Middling**: Users refer informally and we know it happens, but there's no structured mechanism or tracking.
- **Weak**: No referral path exists; word-of-mouth is hoped for, not designed.

### 5. Customer Experience
The end-to-end journey, service quality, and how complaints are handled.
- Self-check: What happens, step by step, when something goes wrong for a customer — is there a defined process or does it depend on who's awake?
- Self-check: Have we ever watched (not asked, watched) a real user go through the full journey?
- Self-check: What is our worst-documented customer experience, and what changed after it happened?
- **Strong**: A defined service standard exists, is monitored, and complaints visibly change the product or process.
- **Middling**: Service is handled well by instinct, but there's no defined standard and it depends on individuals.
- **Weak**: No visibility into the actual end-to-end experience; complaints are handled reactively with no pattern review.

### 6. Narrative Resonance
The brand story, value proposition, and emotional resonance that make people care beyond the transaction.
- Self-check: Can a stranger repeat our story back after hearing it once?
- Self-check: Does our story explain why *we* are the ones solving this, or could any competitor tell the same story?
- Self-check: Have we tested this narrative on someone outside our target segment and watched whether it landed?
- **Strong**: A specific, ownable story that users repeat in their own words when describing us to others.
- **Middling**: A story exists in marketing materials but customers don't spontaneously repeat it.
- **Weak**: No consistent narrative; messaging changes depending on who's writing it that week.

### 7. Brand System
Positioning, personality, tone, and visual consistency across touchpoints.
- Self-check: Would a new touchpoint (email, packaging, support reply) be instantly recognizable as ours without a logo?
- Self-check: Can everyone on the team describe the brand's personality in the same three words?
- Self-check: Is there a written reference anyone can check before creating new material?
- **Strong**: Consistent tone, look, and positioning across every touchpoint, governed by a written reference.
- **Middling**: Brand feels coherent because the founder personally reviews everything, but nothing is documented.
- **Weak**: Tone and visuals vary by whoever produced the material; no shared reference exists.

### 8. Operational Excellence
KPIs, process discipline, financial tracking, and continuous improvement.
- Self-check: Can we produce this week's key numbers in under five minutes, or does it require reconstruction?
- Self-check: Is there a recurring cadence (weekly/monthly) where operating numbers are actually reviewed and acted on?
- Self-check: When something breaks operationally, is there a standard response, or does every incident get solved from scratch?
- **Strong**: A living KPI dashboard reviewed on a fixed cadence, with documented processes for recurring operations.
- **Middling**: Numbers exist and get reviewed occasionally, but not on a fixed cadence and processes live in people's heads.
- **Weak**: No regular metric review; each operational fire is fought independently.

### 9. Community
Community management, engagement, user-generated content, and co-creation.
- Self-check: If we disappeared for a month, would users still be talking to each other about this?
- Self-check: Is there a space (formal or informal) where users interact with each other, not just with us?
- Self-check: Has a user ever created something (content, a use case, an improvement) unprompted?
- **Strong**: An active space where users interact with each other and generate content or ideas without prompting.
- **Middling**: A community channel exists but activity is mostly us posting, with limited peer-to-peer interaction.
- **Weak**: No community surface exists; every interaction is one-to-one between us and a user.

### 10. Visual Communication
Visual design, information graphics, and asset standards.
- Self-check: Could someone unfamiliar with the product understand what it does from the visuals alone?
- Self-check: Is there a documented visual standard, or does quality depend on who made the last asset?
- Self-check: Do our visuals communicate a claim, or just decorate one?
- **Strong**: A documented visual standard producing consistent, comprehension-first assets across the team.
- **Middling**: Visuals look reasonable but quality and consistency vary by creator.
- **Weak**: No standard exists; assets are ad hoc and inconsistent in quality and message.

### 11. Deal-making
Closing conversations, objection handling, pricing strategy.
- Self-check: Can we name our three most common objections and our actual answer to each — not the answer we wish we had?
- Self-check: Is pricing anchored to value delivered, or to what felt easy to say out loud?
- Self-check: What is our actual close rate on qualified conversations, and do we track it?
- **Strong**: A documented, tested objection-handling approach and a pricing rationale tied to value, with tracked close rates.
- **Middling**: Deals close, but through founder charisma rather than a repeatable approach; no tracking.
- **Weak**: No consistent pricing logic or objection handling; every deal is improvised.

### 12. Conversion Engineering
CTA design, funnel optimization, behavioral tracking.
- Self-check: Do we know where in the funnel most prospects drop off, or are we guessing?
- Self-check: When was the last time a specific funnel step was changed based on data rather than opinion?
- Self-check: Is there a single clear next action at every touchpoint, or does the user have to figure out what to do?
- **Strong**: Funnel drop-off points are measured, and specific changes have been made and validated against data.
- **Middling**: Basic tracking exists but changes to the funnel are opinion-driven, not measured.
- **Weak**: No funnel visibility; conversion is treated as a black box.

### Output format

Present as a **radar table** — one row per dimension, one column for the anchor rating (Strong/Middling/Weak), one column for the single most important self-check answer.

Then group into **three time-horizon leverage lanes plus one foundation lane**, and call out the gap in each:

| Lane | Dimensions | What it buys |
|---|---|---|
| **Short-term sprint** | Product & Differentiation, Deal-making, Conversion Engineering | Revenue and traction in the next 90 days |
| **Resilience** | Community, Narrative Resonance, Referral & Revenue-share Mechanics | Ability to survive a slow quarter without collapsing |
| **Long-term compounding** | Brand System, Partnership & Integration, Operational Excellence | Value that gets harder to copy the longer the venture runs |
| **Foundation** (runs under all three) | Compounding Assets, Customer Experience, Visual Communication | The base layer every other lane depends on |

Name explicitly which lane is weakest and whether that weakness is tolerable at this stage (e.g., a pre-launch venture can be forgiven a weak "long-term compounding" lane; it cannot be forgiven a weak "foundation" lane once it has paying customers).

## D2 — People Risk & Equity Structure

Most venture deaths attributed to "the market" are, on closer inspection, co-founder or investor relationship failures that never got named. This overlay exists to name them before they compound. It has two parts: a behavioral red-flag scan for the people you're building with, and a structural equity checklist that prevents the most common written-nowhere disasters.

### Red flag patterns

These are behavior patterns observed in co-founder, investor, and key-partner relationships that precede or accompany equity and control disputes. Present the pattern description; do not accuse — ask the detection questions and let the founder self-assess.

1. **Sovereignty reversal** — Under the banner of "the bigger picture" or "the dream," you're asked to give up a boundary you set. The terms keep shifting, but each shift is framed as being for your own good.
   - Detect: Has a boundary I set been renegotiated more than once, each time framed as necessary for the mission?
   - Detect: If I said no to this request, would the relationship survive intact?

2. **Perception erosion (gaslighting)** — Events get denied or reframed until you start doubting your own memory of what was said or agreed.
   - Detect: Do I find myself keeping written records "just in case" after conversations with this person?
   - Detect: Has anyone else independently confirmed a version of events that this person denies?

3. **Sophistry displacement** — Rhetoric substitutes for evidence. Every concern you raise gets argued away with reasoning that sounds airtight in the moment, and you end up perpetually on the defensive.
   - Detect: After disagreements, do I usually walk away persuaded, or just tired of arguing?
   - Detect: Can this person point to a concrete outcome that supports their position, or only to argumentation?

4. **Intermittent reinforcement** — Hot-and-cold, unpredictable feedback keeps you working harder just to get back to a baseline of approval.
   - Detect: Am I making concessions specifically to "get back in their good graces" rather than because the concession is right on the merits?
   - Detect: Is the warmth in this relationship correlated with anything I can predict, or does it seem random?

5. **Mutual-destruction bluff** — Threats along the lines of "we'll go down together" that make independent decision-making feel unsafe.
   - Detect: Have I avoided making a decision I believe is right because I feared this person's reaction would harm the venture?
   - Detect: Has this person explicitly or implicitly threatened shared consequences to block a decision?

6. **Reality distortion / counter-accusation** — Denial flips into attack: challenges are met with "you don't understand my vision" rather than engagement with the substance.
   - Detect: When I raise a specific, factual concern, does the response address the fact or attack my judgment?
   - Detect: Has a documented commitment been denied outright when I raised it later?

7. **Fast-attachment overtures** — Excessive praise and rapid promises build attachment quickly; once the attachment is formed, the terms change.
   - Detect: Did the warmth and generosity of this relationship precede any real track record together?
   - Detect: Has a term that was "obviously" agreed to early on shifted once I was already committed?

8. **Push-pull oscillation** — "You're the person I trust most" alternates with "you're the one holding us back," with no stable middle state.
   - Detect: Does my standing with this person swing between the two extremes with no in-between?
   - Detect: Can I predict, from the situation alone (not the person's mood), which version I'll get today?

**Defense principle**: none of these patterns are provable from a single incident. The defense is not confrontation — it is documentation (write down what was actually said, when), a third party who can independently verify events, and a standing rule that any material change to roles, equity, or decision rights must be in writing before it takes effect. A relationship that resists putting agreed terms in writing has already told you something.

### Equity structure checklist

Independent of relationship quality, these structural defaults prevent the most common disputes:

- [ ] Vesting is 4 years with a 1-year cliff — applied without exception, including to yourself.
- [ ] An option pool of 10–20% is reserved before finalizing cap table percentages.
- [ ] A near-equal split (e.g., 50/50, or equal three-way) has been explicitly stress-tested against a tie-breaking mechanism — equal is not automatically fair if contributions or commitment levels are not equal, and it removes any clear decision-maker in a deadlock.
- [ ] Co-founders have worked together on something real before formalizing equity — "date before you marry."
- [ ] Four things are in writing, signed, before money or significant work changes hands:
  1. Role and decision rights — who decides what, and what happens when co-founders disagree.
  2. Full-time commitment — by when, and what happens if someone stays part-time longer than agreed.
  3. Departure terms — what happens to unvested and vested equity if someone leaves, for any reason.
  4. Dispute resolution — the process when co-founders disagree, before a disagreement actually happens.

If any box is unchecked, name it explicitly in the diagnostic output as an open structural risk — it does not lower the Survival Index, but it should appear in the Verdict as a flagged item.

## D3 — Strategy Wargame & Stop-loss

A fast strategic-posture read plus a lightweight scenario exercise, meant to surface blind spots a purely financial or product-level analysis will miss — and to force a stop-loss decision to exist *before* it's needed.

### Five-dimension quick assessment

Adapted from classical strategic doctrine, generalized for a business context:

- **Mission** — Does the venture's stated purpose actually align incentives across the team, or is it a slogan nobody would sacrifice for?
  - Quick check: If resources got tight, would the mission tell us what to cut, or is it silent on trade-offs?
- **Timing** — Is the external environment (technology, regulation, behavior) actually moving in this venture's favor right now, and for how long?
  - Quick check: What has to remain true about the outside world for this to keep working, and how fragile is that?
- **Terrain** — What is the actual market structure and channel position the venture is fighting from — favorable ground or a disadvantaged one?
  - Quick check: Are we fighting where the leverage favors us, or where it's simply comfortable?
- **Team** — Does the team's actual composition and capability match what this specific fight requires?
  - Quick check: What capability does winning require that we don't currently have a credible plan to acquire?
- **Discipline** — Is there real governance and execution rigor, or does execution depend entirely on founder attention?
  - Quick check: What happens to execution quality on a week the founder is unavailable?

Score each dimension informally (strong/adequate/weak) and note which dimension is the binding constraint — the one that, if it fails, breaks the others regardless of their individual strength.

### Mini wargame (five steps)

Run this when facing a specific high-stakes decision (a pivot, a competitive threat, a major partnership, a fundraising choice) — not as a routine exercise.

1. **Situation gathering** — State plainly: the battlefield (what market/arena), the objective (what winning looks like), the opponent(s) (who else is affected by or contesting this move), the resources available, and the bottom line (what loss is unacceptable).
2. **Situation diagnosis** — Given the above, what is actually at stake, and what is the real constraint (not the stated one)?
3. **Multi-option generation** — Generate at least two distinct options (not just "do it" vs. "don't"). For each option, state: the upside, the cost, the risk, and the concrete next step if chosen.
4. **Wargaming the response** — For each option, play out the most likely reaction from the opponent/market, and identify the tipping point where the situation would clearly be going wrong.
5. **Rollback and stop-loss** — For the option selected, write the specific, observable condition under which the venture will reverse course, and what the reversal actually looks like operationally. This step is mandatory, not optional.

**Rule**: if step 5 produces no concrete stop-loss clause — no specific, observable trigger and no defined reversal action — the Verdict output must carry a red flag. This does not trigger a Kill in the scored gauntlet, but it must be visible at the top-line judgment: a venture proceeding into execution with no defined exit condition is one unrecognized failure mode away from riding a bad decision to zero.
