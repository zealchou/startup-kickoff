# Founder OS

> This is the founder's self-management layer — referenced by the Kickoff Package (🏆 verdict only) and by G12 (Founder-Market Fit) in the scored gauntlet. It does not score ventures; it equips the founder who is about to run one. Four components: a self-correction loop for the founder's own operating state, a resource audit that makes "unfair advantage" concrete instead of aspirational, a founder-led selling framework for the pre-team-of-salespeople stage, and an end-to-end customer journey audit.

---

## 1. The Perception-Action-Feedback Loop

The founding insight this generalizes: behavior is not aimed at producing an action for its own sake — it is aimed at closing the gap between what you perceive and what you want to perceive. A founder under stress who is snapping at the team is not "being difficult"; some perceived state (control, respect, progress) is off-target, and the snapping is the (poor) action being taken to close that gap. The practical implication is significant: a founder does not need to control people to get a venture to run well. The founder needs to design the perception and feedback mechanisms so that the *system* — team, product, market signal — self-corrects toward the target state, the same way a thermostat doesn't argue with the room, it just measures and adjusts.

This reframes founder work from "manage people harder" to "design better signal loops":

- What is the perception this person/team/system is actually operating on? (Not what you told them — what they're perceving.)
- What feedback do they currently get, and how fast?
- Is the feedback loop tight enough that self-correction can happen before a small gap becomes a large one?

### Weekly three questions

A minimal-viable version of this loop the founder runs on themselves, every week, in writing:

1. **What was the most energizing moment this week?** (Signal: where is the perceived state closest to the target — protect and repeat this.)
2. **What was the most draining moment this week?** (Signal: where is the gap largest, or where is the founder taking action without a working feedback loop.)
3. **What will I keep, drop, and add next week?** (The actual course-correction — this question is worthless without the previous two being answered honestly first.)

### The five-step gap model

For any specific stuck situation (not just the weekly review), the same loop expands into five steps:

1. **Current state** — What is actually true right now, stated without editorializing.
2. **Target state** — What perception/outcome is actually wanted (be specific — "better" is not a target state).
3. **The gap** — The precise, nameable difference between 1 and 2.
4. **Options** — What actions could close that specific gap (not generic "work harder" — actions that map to the gap named in step 3).
5. **Resources** — What is needed to execute the chosen option, cross-referenced against the Resource Audit below.

**Worked illustration** (generic, not tied to any specific venture): A founder notices the team keeps missing a weekly deadline. The reflexive action is to add a stricter check-in. Run the loop instead: *Current state* — deadlines are missed roughly half the time. *Target state* — the team hits the deadline without the founder chasing it. *Gap* — the team isn't missing the deadline out of low effort; they're missing it because priorities get silently reshuffled mid-week by requests the founder isn't aware of. *Options* — either the founder stops introducing new requests mid-week, or a visible priority queue is introduced so reshuffling is explicit instead of silent. *Resources* — this requires no new headcount or capital, just a change to the founder's own Time and Discipline categories in the Resource Audit below. Note what didn't happen: the founder didn't need to "manage harder" — the fix was in the feedback loop, not in more control.

## 2. Founder Resource Audit

"Unfair advantage" is a phrase every startup framework uses and almost none makes operational. This audit breaks it into eight concrete categories of leverage a founder either has, is building, or needs a credible plan to acquire. Score each 0–2 (0 = none, 1 = some but underdeveloped, 2 = a genuine current advantage) and reference this directly in G12 (Founder-Market Fit) of the scored gauntlet — a founder with no score above 0 across all eight categories, and no credible plan to acquire one, is a Kill signal in G12, not a soft note.

- **Audience** — An existing owned audience (newsletter, social following, community) the founder can reach directly without paying for it.
  - Self-assess: If I published something today, how many people who don't already know me personally would see it, and would any of them act on it?
  - 0: No owned audience; every reach requires paying or someone else's platform. 1: A small or lukewarm audience exists but engagement is thin. 2: A sizeable, engaged audience will visibly act (click, reply, buy) when the founder asks.
- **Network** — People the founder can actually call and get a response from — not LinkedIn connections, people who will take the call.
  - Self-assess: Name five people I could call this week who would meaningfully help this venture, and would take the call.
  - 0: Fewer than two such people exist. 1: A handful exist but their help would be marginal to this specific venture. 2: Multiple people who could materially move this venture (customers, hires, capital, doors) will take the call today.
- **Platform** — A position inside an organization, community, or structure that gives the founder distribution or mobilization power beyond their individual reach.
  - Self-assess: Is there a role, seat, or position I currently hold that gives me reach or credibility this venture wouldn't otherwise have?
  - 0: No such position exists. 1: A position exists but its reach is tangential to this venture's target customer. 2: The position gives direct, credible access to the exact audience this venture needs.
- **Credibility** — Trust earned through a track record of saying and doing consistent things over time, in this domain or an adjacent one.
  - Self-assess: If a stranger checked my public track record, would it support or undercut the claim I'm making with this venture?
  - 0: No public track record exists, or it undercuts the claim. 1: A track record exists but is unrelated to this venture's domain. 2: A verifiable track record directly supports the specific claim this venture is making.
- **Domain knowledge** — Depth of understanding in the specific problem space, typically built over five or more years of direct exposure.
  - Self-assess: Do I understand this problem well enough to know which "obvious" solutions actually fail, and why?
  - 0: Less than a year of direct exposure to this problem space. 1: Some exposure, but the founder is still learning which assumptions are wrong. 2: Five or more years of direct exposure; the founder can name specific "obvious" approaches that fail and why.
- **R&D** — Cross-domain research or building capability that lets the founder combine ideas or execute technically in ways competitors can't easily replicate.
  - Self-assess: Is there a capability I have — technical, creative, analytical — that most people attempting this venture would not have?
  - 0: No distinguishing capability; the founder would build this the same way any competent operator would. 1: Some distinguishing capability exists but hasn't yet translated into the product or offer. 2: A specific, hard-to-replicate capability is already embedded in what's being built.
- **Time** — Team bandwidth actually freed up through skill and delegation, not time nominally "available" on a calendar.
  - Self-assess: If I needed 20 focused hours this week for this venture, could I actually get them, or would something else eat them first?
  - 0: No dedicated time exists; this venture competes with a full-time job or caregiving load with no slack. 1: Some dedicated time exists but is frequently displaced by other obligations. 2: Meaningful, protected, recurring time is genuinely available.
- **Capital** — Money on hand, or a credible, tested path to raising or accessing it.
  - Self-assess: If this venture needed $50k in the next 90 days, do I have a real path to it, or only an assumption that "it'll work out"?
  - 0: No capital on hand and no tested path to raising any. 1: Some capital exists or a path is plausible but untested. 2: Capital is on hand, or a specific, previously-used path to raising it is available.

A founder scoring low across most categories is not disqualified — but the gauntlet expects them to name, explicitly, which category they intend to build first and how, rather than proceeding as if the advantage already exists. A founder scoring 0 on every category, with no stated plan to move even one to a 1, is the concrete signal behind a G12 Kill: "no unfair advantage and no plan to acquire one."

## 3. Founder-Led Selling: Four Stages

Before a venture can afford a sales team, the founder is the sales team — and how they sell in this phase sets the tone the eventual team will inherit. Four stages, each with three operating points.

**Ethical boundary, stated up front**: this framework is for surfacing a real problem and offering a real choice — not for manufacturing urgency, exploiting insecurity, or pressuring someone past their actual judgment. If a technique here only works on someone who is confused or anxious, it is being misused. The close in stage four is explicitly options-based, not pressure-based, for this reason.

### Stage 1 — Build trust
- Establish the relationship context honestly: how you found each other, why this conversation is happening now.
- Understand their situation before pitching anything — the goal of this stage is orientation, not persuasion.
- Demonstrate competence through specific, relevant knowledge of their situation, not generic credentials.
- *Watch for misuse*: fabricating a shared connection or false context to manufacture rapport. If the "how we found each other" story isn't true, this stage has already broken the ethical boundary.

### Stage 2 — Surface the real pain
- Go past the stated problem to the underlying one — ask what happens if this doesn't get solved, and then ask what happens after that.
- Let them articulate the cost of the problem in their own words rather than supplying it for them — a cost you name is forgettable; a cost they name is not.
- Respond with genuine understanding of the difficulty, not a premature pivot to your solution.
- *Watch for misuse*: pushing past a genuine "no, it's not that bad" to manufacture a pain that isn't actually there. Surfacing a real pain is not the same as inventing one.

### Stage 3 — Ignite commitment
- Make the gap between their current state and their desired state vivid and specific, using their own words from stage 2.
- Connect emotionally to why solving this now matters, using a concrete example or comparable case — not abstract benefits.
- Confirm they actually want to close this gap, in their own words, before presenting any solution.
- *Watch for misuse*: manufacturing false urgency ("only available today") to short-circuit their own confirmation step. If urgency isn't real, don't state it.

### Stage 4 — Close with options
- Present the value of what's being offered relative to the cost of the problem named in stage 2 — not relative to a generic price anchor.
- Offer 2–3 concrete options rather than a single take-it-or-leave-it choice, so the decision is which path, not whether to decide.
- Close the loop with an explicit commitment — a specific next action, owned by a specific date — rather than leaving the conversation open-ended.
- *Watch for misuse*: framing "no" as not actually an option, or making the options asymmetric on purpose (one deliberately bad option to force the other). A legitimate option-based close can survive the prospect choosing none of them.

## 4. End-to-End Customer Journey Audit

The most common founder blind spot is confusing "we built a process" with "we built a system a customer actually experiences well." A process is a sequence of internal steps; a system is that sequence as experienced, end to end, from the outside. This audit walks the seven steps of the customer's actual journey — not the internal org chart's version of it.

1. **Attract** — How does a prospective customer first encounter this venture, and what impression does that first encounter create?
   - Self-check: If I only had this first encounter to judge by, would I trust this enough to take the next step?
2. **Enter** — What happens in the first minute after they decide to engage — is entry frictionless, or does the system make them work to get in?
   - Self-check: How many steps or how much information stands between "I'm interested" and "I'm in"? Is each one earning its cost?
3. **Browse / choose the menu** — Can they see their options clearly, or are they guessing at what's available and what it costs?
   - Self-check: Could a first-time visitor explain what their options are and what each one costs, without asking a human?
4. **Choose** — When they pick something, is the decision easy to make and easy to confirm, or does uncertainty creep in at the moment of commitment?
   - Self-check: At the exact moment of commitment, what doubt might still be in their head, and does anything in the system address it?
5. **Serve** — Is what was promised actually delivered, on the timeline and to the standard implied at the point of choosing?
   - Self-check: Compare what was promised at step 4, word for word, against what was actually delivered — where's the gap?
6. **Experience** — Beyond the transaction itself, how does the surrounding experience feel — supported, ignored, delighted, tolerated?
   - Self-check: If this customer described the experience to a friend in one sentence, what would that sentence be?
7. **Return** — Is there a reason and a clear path for them to come back, or does the relationship end by default once the transaction closes?
   - Self-check: What specifically happens after delivery — is there a next touchpoint, or does the system simply go quiet?

Run this by actually walking the journey as a customer would — not by describing it from memory. Gaps found here are frequently invisible from inside the organization and are a leading cause of churn that "the product is good" analyses miss entirely.

### Common failure mode per step

A quick reference for what "broken" typically looks like at each step, useful when auditing an existing venture rather than designing a new one:

| Step | Typical failure |
|---|---|
| Attract | The first impression sets an expectation the rest of the journey doesn't meet. |
| Enter | Unnecessary friction (forced signup, unclear first action) loses people who were already interested. |
| Browse | Options and pricing are ambiguous, forcing a support conversation just to understand what's on offer. |
| Choose | Doubt at the moment of commitment goes unaddressed, so the prospect abandons at the last step. |
| Serve | Delivery quietly falls short of what was promised, and no one internally notices because no one compares the two. |
| Experience | The transaction is fine but the surrounding experience is forgettable, so there is nothing to talk about afterward. |
| Return | Nothing prompts a second interaction, so single-purchase customers never become repeat ones by default. |

## How the four pieces connect

These four components are not independent checklists — they're a single operating stack, read top to bottom:

- The **Perception-Action-Feedback loop** is the founder's own operating system: it's what should be running underneath every other section, catching drift before it compounds.
- The **Resource Audit** is an honest inventory of what the founder actually has to work with — it should directly inform which options look viable in step 4 of the gap model above.
- **Founder-led selling** is the loop and the resources applied to the single highest-leverage activity in an early venture: getting someone to say yes. It is where Audience, Network, and Credibility from the Resource Audit get spent, not just counted.
- The **customer journey audit** is where the founder finds out, empirically, whether the selling and the delivery actually match — closing the loop back to step 1's core claim: design the feedback mechanism, don't just push harder.

A founder who runs the weekly three questions but never walks the customer journey is optimizing their own state while flying blind on the system's actual output. A founder who audits the journey obsessively but never runs the weekly loop will burn out before the fixes land. Both halves are required.
