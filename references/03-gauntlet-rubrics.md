# Gauntlet Rubrics — 12 Gates, Full Scoring Specification

Every gate follows the same anatomy: **Purpose → Sub-criteria (MECE) → Scoring
anchors (0–5) → Kill conditions → Refutation angles** (where the devil's
advocate should attack first). Universal rules:

- Score against anchors, not vibes. If a score lands between anchors, take the
  lower one and say why.
- **C-tier-only evidence caps any gate at 2/5.**
- A Kill condition firing at any gate ☠️-kills every assumption hanging on it
  and triggers the index cap (Ring 1 → 29%; Rings 2–4 → 54%).
- A refutation may only trigger a Kill if the refutation itself is A/B-tier.
- **Absence-based Kills** (e.g., G5 "no why-now answer", G11 "no security
  plan"): the founder must get ONE explicit chance to answer — at intake or at
  the gate itself — before the Kill fires. A question asked and not answered
  is *demonstrated absence*: a verifiable fact of the record, and therefore
  A-tier evidence. A question never asked is not.

Scoring anchor convention used at every gate:
- **0** — the dimension is absent or actively contradicted by evidence.
- **1–2** — asserted only (C-tier), or evidence is anecdotal/conflicting.
- **3** — B-tier support: coherent, sourced inference; no empirical contact yet.
- **4** — at least one strand of A-tier evidence corroborates the B-tier case.
- **5** — multiple independent A-tier strands; the refutation was attempted
  with real evidence and failed.

## RING 1 — PROBLEM & CUSTOMER (weight 35%)

The ring that kills most startups: ~42% of post-mortems cite no market need
(CB Insights). A Ring-1 Kill caps the Survival Index at 29%.

### G1 · Problem Reality & Testability

**Purpose:** establish that a real, specific, frequent, severe problem exists
and that the founder's claim about it is falsifiable.

**Sub-criteria (MECE):**
1. *Specificity* — the problem statement names an actor, a context, a
   frequency, and a cost. ("Contract review takes too long" fails; "in-house
   legal teams at mid-market companies spend 3+ days per review cycle because
   redlines live in email threads" passes.)
2. *Severity & frequency* — the pain is a top-3 problem for the actor, or
   frequent enough that a mediocre-severity pain compounds.
3. *Falsifiability* — a defined observation could prove the hypothesis wrong.
4. *Evidence of pursuit* — people already spend money, time, or hacked-together
   workarounds on it (a problem nobody attempts to solve is usually not felt).

**Kill conditions:**
- The hypothesis cannot be phrased falsifiably after one reformulation attempt.
- The founder cannot name who has the problem, how often, or how badly.

**Refutation angles:** argue the pain is a vitamin not a painkiller; argue the
observation is real but the root cause is different (making the solution moot);
argue frequency is episodic (annual pain can't sustain a product).

### G2 · Target Customer & Reachability

**Purpose:** a market is not "who could use this" but "who can you actually
reach, with a name-able profile."

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Profile precision* — title/role, organization type and size, seniority;
   for consumer: situation, trigger moment, spending pattern.
2. *Reachability* — named watering holes (communities, channels, events,
   lists) where this profile actually congregates and can be contacted.
3. *Buyer clarity* — who holds budget vs. who feels the pain vs. who
   influences; if these differ, the founder knows the chain.

**Kill conditions:**
- Target customer is "everyone," "SMBs," or any segment so broad it cannot
  yield an interview list of 20 named prospects within a week.

**Refutation angles:** argue the profile that feels the pain has no budget;
argue the reachable segment is not the one with the problem; argue the
interview list would actually be friends and existing fans (sampling bias).

### G3 · Existing Alternatives & Switching

**Purpose:** the enemy is rarely a competitor — it's the current workaround
plus inertia.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Current-solution map* — what the target uses today (including spreadsheets,
   interns, "just living with it") and what it truly costs them.
2. *Switching trigger* — a concrete event or threshold that would make them
   change (new regulation, scale break, budget cycle).
3. *Switching cost honesty* — data migration, retraining, workflow rewiring,
   political risk of championing a new tool; the wedge must beat the *total*
   cost of change, not just the sticker price.

**Kill conditions:**
- The current solution is free AND good enough — no felt gap to wedge into.
- Past-tense evidence (Mom-Test style) shows targets describing the problem
  but taking zero action to solve it, repeatedly.

**Refutation angles:** argue "good enough" wins by default; argue the pain is
real but delegated (the person who feels it isn't the one who'd buy); argue
your 10x improvement is on a dimension users don't rank.

## RING 2 — MARKET & TIMING (weight 25%)

### G4 · Market Size (bottom-up)

**Purpose:** size the market as a reasoning tool, not a slide decoration.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Bottom-up SOM* — (# reachable target customers) × (plausible ARPU) ×
   (realistic obtainable share, 1–3 yr). This is the number that must be
   defended; top-down industry reports are only a sanity check.
2. *Assumption exposure* — the three most fragile inputs of the model are
   named and stress-tested (halve them: does the business still exist?).
3. *Market direction* — expanding / consolidating / mature, with implications
   (expanding rewards speed; mature rewards differentiation).

**Kill conditions:**
- Bottom-up SOM, even at the founder's own optimistic inputs, cannot sustain
  minimum viable revenue (the founder's stated survival threshold — ramen
  profitability or lifestyle floor — or, for a venture-scale claim, a credible
  path past $100M revenue).

**Refutation angles:** rebuild the founder's TAM bottom-up and show the gap;
argue the "addressable" population confuses users with buyers; argue market
math depends on a behavior change the market hasn't shown.

### G5 · Why Now

**Purpose:** the most under-asked question in diligence. No enabling shift
usually means too early (market unready) or too late (already taken).

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Enabling shift* — a named technology, regulation, cost-curve, or behavior
   change that makes this possible/necessary now and wasn't true 3–5 yrs ago.
2. *Graveyard scan* — who tried this before, why did they fail, and which of
   those failure causes has actually changed?
3. *Tailwind/headwind audit* — three external trends (regulatory, technical,
   demographic) rated as tailwind or headwind for the next 24 months.

**Kill conditions:**
- No articulable answer to "why is this now possible/necessary when it wasn't
  before" after the graveyard scan.

**Refutation angles:** argue the enabling shift enables every competitor
equally (it's a tide, not a moat); argue the graveyard died of a cause that
still holds; argue the window already closed with the current leader.

### G6 · Competitive Landscape

**Purpose:** counter competitor neglect — founders systematically underrate
others while overrating their own vision.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Four-tier map* — direct competitors / indirect & workaround substitutes /
   adjacent players who could pivot in / potential acquirers already circling
   the space.
2. *Steelman round* — for the strongest player in each relevant tier, the most
   convincing case that THEY win and you lose (not the easiest-to-dismiss
   version).
3. *Counter-positioning* — a reason incumbents structurally can't or won't
   copy the wedge (business-model conflict, channel conflict), not just
   "we'll move faster."

**Kill conditions:**
- The founder claims "no competitors" (it means no research or no market —
  both fatal). The status quo always competes.

**Refutation angles:** the steelman IS the refutation at this gate; also argue
the incumbent's distribution beats your product delta ("incumbents get
innovation before startups get distribution").

## RING 3 — BUSINESS ENGINE (weight 20%)

### G7 · Unit Economics & Pricing

**Purpose:** scale only amplifies the sign of unit economics. Check the sign
before dreaming of the magnitude.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Value-based price hypothesis* — price anchored to the customer's cost of
   the problem (money/time currently burned), not to founder cost or
   competitor sticker; a chosen value metric that scales with delivered value.
2. *CAC realism* — acquisition cost estimated from the actual proposed
   channel's known economics, not asserted.
3. *LTV integrity* — LTV built from gross margin and a churn assumption the
   founder can defend; healthy references: LTV:CAC ≥ 3, payback < 12 months
   (label as reference ranges, not laws).
4. *Willingness-to-pay plan* — a concrete test (pre-sales, LOIs, paid pilots,
   price-sensitivity interviews with the economic buyer).

**Kill conditions:**
- LTV:CAC < 1 under the founder's own optimistic scenario.

**Refutation angles:** argue the value metric charges for something users
expect free; argue churn assumption contradicts the switching-cost story told
at G3 (low switching cost cuts both ways); argue the price point forces an
enterprise sales motion the founder can't afford (links to G9).

### G8 · Channel & Growth

**Purpose:** a product without a repeatable path to customers is a hobby with
a burn rate.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *First channel hypothesis* — one named channel where the target profile
   already congregates, with a testable cost/conversion guess.
2. *Post-heroics engine* — what growth looks like after founder-led selling
   and launch-spike energy (Product Hunt, friends, investor intros) decay;
   early traction sources are explicitly labeled non-repeatable.
3. *Channel–product fit* — price point supports the channel's CAC (high-touch
   sales needs high ACV; viral needs a sharing-native product).

**Kill conditions:**
- Growth plan amounts to "build it and they will come" / "it will go viral"
  with no mechanism.

**Refutation angles:** argue the channel is saturated or rented land
(platform-dependency risk); argue the growth loop requires density the cold
start can't reach; argue founder-led selling is masking channel absence.

### G9 · Capital & Runway

**Purpose:** unit economics can be healthy while the company still dies of
cash before proving them. Diligence-grade table stakes.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Cost-to-validation* — realistic capital needed to reach the next hard
   evidence milestone (problem-solution fit → PMF), with AI-era honesty: build
   cost is down, but founder living costs, distribution, and time are not zero.
2. *Default alive/dead* — on current spend and plausible revenue curve, does
   the idea reach break-even before cash runs out? If default dead, the plan
   says which lever moves (revenue acceleration or cost cut).
3. *Funding path realism* — the assumed capital source (savings, revenue,
   angels, VC) matched against what that source actually funds at this stage
   and in this sector.

**Kill conditions:**
- The validation plan requires capital with no realistic acquisition path.
- The survival plan is structurally "the next round will save us" — outsourcing
  default-dead to future fundraising.

**Refutation angles:** argue the timeline to PMF is 2–3x the founder's
estimate (it usually is); argue this sector's validation is capital-heavy
(hardware, regulated, marketplace cold-start) and the lean-AI narrative
doesn't transfer.

## RING 4 — BUILDABILITY & EXECUTION (weight 20%)

The AI-native ring. Building is cheap now — which moves the risk, not removes it.

### G10 · Solution Feasibility

**Purpose:** "can this be built" is now really "can THIS founder, with agentic
tools, build the core interaction — and are its dependencies actually
available?"

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Core interaction definition* — the single interaction the solution's value
   depends on, isolated (this is also the prototype spec).
2. *Dependency audit* — the data, APIs, integrations, licenses, or supply the
   core interaction needs: available, at what cost, with what platform risk?
3. *Founder+AI buildability* — can a lean team ship the core interaction with
   agentic coding in weeks? If specialists are unavoidable (regulated ML,
   hardware), that cost re-enters G9.
4. *Solution–problem lock* — the design solves the problem validation
   revealed, not the problem originally imagined; the three assumptions the
   design leans on hardest are named.

**Kill conditions:**
- The core interaction depends on technology, data access, or licenses that
  are unavailable or unproven at any price the business could bear.

**Refutation angles:** argue the demo-able version and the reliable version
are different products (the last 20% is the product); argue the key data
dependency is revocable by a platform whose interests diverge; argue the
"AI does X" claim exceeds current model reliability for the stakes involved.

### G11 · Launch Responsibility — Security, AI Tech Debt, Compliance

**Purpose:** AI writes code that works, not code that is safe. Security flaws
are invisible until exploited — there is no natural feedback loop. And AI tech
debt compounds: without written specs and architecture constraints, every
session re-derives decisions and drifts.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Data exposure map* — what user data is held; what a breach would mean;
   pre-launch security review (auth/session, data exposure, input validation,
   dependency audit) planned as the minimum responsible threshold.
2. *Tech-debt hygiene plan* — persistent context (spec, architecture
   constraints, scope contract, CLAUDE.md-style memory) planned from day one,
   so the codebase keeps a coherent mental model.
3. *Regulatory scan* — does this touch a regulated domain (health, finance,
   children, hiring, privacy-heavy)? Named compliance requirements and a path
   (or a deliberate, argued decision that none apply).

**Kill conditions:**
- Handles real user data with no pre-launch security review plan.
- Operates in a regulated industry with no compliance path at all.

**Refutation angles:** argue the compliance burden inverts unit economics
(links to G7/G9); argue the founder's "we'll fix security later" meets real
users on day one; argue the vertical's buyers demand certifications
(SOC 2, HIPAA) that gate every sale.

### G12 · Founder–Market Fit

**Purpose:** ideas are cheap and now buildable by anyone — so "why you" is a
larger share of the remaining risk, not a smaller one.

**Sub-criteria:**
1. *Unfair advantage* — domain depth, audience, network, proprietary insight
   from lived experience — something not erased by the same AI tools everyone
   else has.
2. *Resource audit* — the eight founder leverages (audience, network,
   platform position, credibility, domain knowledge, R&D ability, time,
   capital — see `05-founder-os.md`), scored 0–2 each; the gate cares about
   the shape, not the sum: which leverages does THIS idea require, and does
   the founder hold or have a plan to acquire those specific ones?
3. *Obsession horizon* — evidence the founder will still care about this
   problem in year 3 (proximity to the pain, history with it); most deaths
   route through founder abandonment.

**Kill conditions:**
- No unfair advantage of any kind AND no credible plan to acquire one.

**Refutation angles:** argue the founder's advantage is generic ("I'm
technical" no longer differentiates); argue the required leverage for this
idea (e.g., enterprise network) is exactly the one missing; argue proximity:
the founder has never been, met, or sold to the target customer.

## Aggregation Reference

- Assumption verdicts: ✅ = 1.0 · 🩸 = 0.5 · ☠️ = 0.0, weighted by the ring of
  the gate each assumption hangs on (35/25/20/20).
- Survival Index = weighted survived mass ÷ weighted total mass, expressed as
  a percentage, always labeled "directional indicator, not a probability."
- Kill caps: Ring-1 Kill → cap 29% · any other Kill → cap 54%. Caps do not
  stack; the lowest applicable cap wins.
- Tier from final capped index: ☠️ <30 · 🚑 30–54 · 🛠 55–74 · 🏆 ≥75.
- Ring weights are a prior from startup mortality data (no-market-need
  dominant), not ground truth — disclose this when asked and never present
  the index with decimal places (false precision).
